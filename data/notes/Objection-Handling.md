# Objection Handling - Turn No into Yes

## The Core Principle

**Objections are not rejection. They're requests for more information.**

When someone objects, they're actually saying:
- "I don't have enough information yet"
- "I don't trust you enough yet"
- "I don't see the value clearly yet"

Your job: Help them get what they need.

---

## The 3-Step Objection Framework

### Step 1: Acknowledge & Validate
Show you heard them and understand their concern.

```
"I completely understand..."
"That's a valid concern..."
"I hear that a lot..."
```

### Step 2: Isolate & Clarify
Uncover the real issue behind the objection.

```
"Is it the total amount, or the monthly budget?"
"Is the timing the issue, or something else?"
"What would need to be different for this to work?"
```

### Step 3: Respond & Check
Address the concern, then confirm.

```
"So if we could [solution], would that work for you?"
"Does that address your concern?"
"How does that sound?"
```

## Common Objections & Scripts

### "It's too expensive"

**What they really mean**: "I don't see enough value yet."

**Don't defend the price**. Build the value.

```
"I hear that. Can you tell me what you're comparing to?

You know, a lot of our clients initially felt the same way.
But what they found was that [specific ROI/result],
and it actually paid for itself in [timeframe].

What result would you need to see to feel this is a good investment?"
```

**For Indonesian market** (see [[Local-Business-Optimization]]):
```
"I understand. For a small business, every rupiah counts.

Let me break it down differently: This costs about 16K per day.
If it brings you even one new customer per month,
you're already profitable.

What's one customer worth to you?"
```

### "I need to think about it"

**What they really mean**: "I'm not ready to decide yet."

```
"Totally understand. This is an important decision.

What specifically would you like to think about?
Is it the fit, the timing, or something else?

You know, I find it helpful when people write down
their top 3 questions or concerns. Would that help?
I can answer those for you right now."
```

**The alternative**:
```
"Of course. Take your time.

I'll send you a summary of everything we discussed
plus a few case studies from similar businesses.

Would Thursday or Friday be better for a quick follow-up?"
```

### "Let me check with my partner/boss"

**What they really mean**: "I'm not the only decision maker."

```
"Absolutely. It's smart to get alignment.

What information would be helpful for that conversation?
I can put together a one-page summary with the key points,
investment, and timeline to make it easier.

Who else should be part of this conversation?"
```

### "We're already working with someone"

**What they really mean**: "Switching is a hassle."

```
"That's great that you have someone helping you.

Without prying, can I ask: What's working well
with your current setup? And what's not?

I'm not asking you to switch—I just want to understand.
Sometimes having a conversation helps clarify what you really need.

And if you ever need a backup plan, you know where to find me."
```

### "Your competitor is cheaper"

**What they really mean**: "I don't see why you cost more."

```
"They do offer a lower price, you're right.

Here's what that usually means: [explain what's missing].

You know, I could offer a cheaper version too.
But here's what gets cut out: [list what they'd lose].

Is that something you'd want to compromise on,
or is the full service worth the difference?"
```

### "I don't have the budget right now"

**What they really mean**: "It's not a priority" OR "Not at this price."

```
"I completely understand. Budgets are real.

Help me understand: Is this a 'not now' or a 'not ever' situation?

If it's timing, we could start smaller and expand later.
Or if it's priority, what would need to change
for this to become more important?

What are your top 3 priorities right now?"
```

### "Send me a proposal"

**What they really mean**: "I want to get off the phone" OR "I need more info."

```
"I'd be happy to put together a proposal.

But proposals work best when they're tailored to your specific situation.
Can we spend 5 more minutes so I ask a few questions?

That way, the proposal will actually address what you care about
instead of being generic. Does that sound fair?"
```

**If they insist**:
```
"Absolutely. I'll send it over by tomorrow.

Just to set expectations: Most proposals I send
get about a 5-minute skim. Can we block 15 minutes
on [day] to review it together and discuss any questions?"
```

## The Feel, Felt, Found Technique

For objections about your approach, product, or company.

```
FEEL: "I understand how you feel."
FELT: "Several of our best clients felt the same way at first."
FOUND: "But what they found was that once they tried it,
       they saw [specific result]."

Does that example resonate with your situation?"
```

---

## The Pre-emptive Strike

Address objections before they come up.

```
"Now, you might be thinking this is a significant investment.
And you'd be right—it is.

But here's why our clients see it differently: [value prop].

Is that concern you had, or is there something else?"
```

---

## When Objections Are Actually Questions

Sometimes objections are just questions with attitude.

### "That's expensive" → "What's included?"
### "That sounds complicated" → "How does it work?"
### "I'm not sure" → "Can you explain more?"

**Your response**: Answer the question underneath the objection.

```
"That's a fair question. Here's what's included: [breakdown].

Does that help clarify the value?"
```

---

## The Pivot: When to Accept No

Sometimes, "no" really means "no."

**Signs it's time to walk away**:
- They won't give you straight answers
- They're disrespectful of your time
- They keep moving the goalposts
- Your gut says this will be a nightmare client

**How to exit gracefully**:

```
"I appreciate your time today.

Based on our conversation, I'm not sure we're the right fit
for what you need right now.

I'd be happy to revisit in the future if things change.
Let's stay connected on [LinkedIn/email]."

Wish them the best—genuinely.
```
